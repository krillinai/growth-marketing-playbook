# Positioning

> Positioning defines the competitive context in which a specific product delivers differentiated value to a specific best-fit customer.

**[English](README.md)｜[简体中文](README.zh.md)**

## Definitions and Boundaries

| Concept | Primary question | Boundary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Positioning | Why is this product the best choice for this customer in this situation? | It is a market hypothesis, not a slogan. |
| Messaging | How should the position be expressed to a particular audience and moment? | Messaging can vary by channel while the underlying position stays coherent. |
| Value proposition | Which valuable outcome is promised, for whom, and why is it credible? | It is one part of positioning and does not define the full competitive context. |
| Brand | What expectations, memory, identity, and trust accumulate around the company or product? | Brand can reinforce a position but does not replace customer and competitive evidence. |
| Strategy | Where and how will the company compete, allocate resources, and build advantage? | Strategy chooses actions; positioning explains the current basis of choice. |
| Vision | What future does the company seek to create? | Vision can guide direction but may not explain why a buyer should choose now. |
| Category | Which market frame gives buyers assumptions about alternatives and evaluation criteria? | A category is a positioning decision, not the whole position. |
| Segmentation and ICP | Which customer differences change the product or growth decision? | Segmentation defines fit conditions; positioning explains differentiated value within them. |

Positioning sits between Product-Market Fit evidence and growth execution:

```text
product, customer, and competitive evidence
-> positioning hypothesis
-> messages, narrative, offer, and qualification
-> acquisition, sales, activation, pricing, and retention behavior
-> revised evidence and positioning
```

Positioning cannot manufacture demand, product quality, retention, or willingness to pay. It makes the assumptions visible enough to test.

## Positioning Unit, Scope, and Version

Define the unit before running a positioning exercise. A company can hold different valid positions for different products, segments, buying situations, geographies, or routes to market.

| Scope field | Decision |
| --- | --- |
| Product unit | Product, edition, module, platform, service, or bundle |
| Customer unit | User, team, account, buyer, industry, use case, or market tier |
| Buying situation | Trigger, job, urgency, current alternative, and decision criteria |
| Geography and language | Market conditions, regulation, culture, local alternatives, and terminology |
| Route to market | Self-serve, sales-led, partner-led, marketplace, or hybrid |
| Time horizon | Current position for the next operating period, not an eternal identity |
| Owner and version | Named decision owner, evidence date, approved version, and next review |

A useful position is specific enough to guide decisions and broad enough to remain coherent across the intended journey. If one statement must cover incompatible buyers or products, split the scope before simplifying the language.

## Competitive Alternatives

Start with what customers would do if the product did not exist. Alternatives include:

- a direct competitor;
- an adjacent or general-purpose product;
- spreadsheets, documents, email, or internal tools;
- manual work or an outsourced service;
- an existing process, behavior, or budget allocation;
- delay, inaction, or accepting the problem.

Use win/loss interviews, replacement behavior, search and sales language, procurement comparisons, churn destinations, and observed workflows. Competitor feature grids are supporting material, not the primary evidence.

Map alternatives by buying situation rather than assuming one global competitor set:

| Alternative | Why customers choose it | Where it performs well | Where it breaks | Evidence status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Status quo | No switching cost or new risk | Familiar workflow and ownership | Existing cost, delay, error, or constraint remains | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Direct competitor | Familiar category and evaluation criteria | Known capabilities and proof | Relevant gap for the target situation | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| General tool | Flexible and already available | Low incremental cost | Setup, governance, specialization, or scale limits | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Service or manual work | Human judgment and customization | Complex or low-frequency tasks | Cost, speed, consistency, or capacity limits | Verified, inferred, or missing |

Inaction is often the strongest alternative. If the cost of change exceeds the recognized cost of the problem, differentiation against competitors will not create urgency.

## Unique Capabilities, Differentiated Value, and Proof

Positioning requires a chain from a real difference to a customer outcome:

```text
unique capability
-> operational or behavioral consequence
-> differentiated customer value
-> proof
-> conditions and limits
```

Capabilities can come from product design, proprietary data, workflow integration, network structure, distribution, service, operations, trust, compliance, ecosystem, speed, cost structure, or a combination competitors cannot reproduce under the same conditions.

| Layer | Test |
| --- | --- |
| Capability | Does the product provide this now, and can alternatives match it under the same conditions? |
| Consequence | What changes in the customer's work or behavior because of the capability? |
| Value | Does that change affect revenue, cost, time, risk, quality, access, control, or strategic flexibility? |
| Proof | What product demonstration, customer outcome, technical evidence, benchmark, or operational record supports it? |
| Boundary | For which customer, workflow, scale, geography, or prerequisite does the claim stop being true? |

Avoid differences that are only roadmap claims and benefits every competitor can assert, such as “easy,” “modern,” “powerful,” or “AI-powered.” A stronger proof chain states the conditions under which the value can be observed.

## Best-Fit Customers and Segmentation

Best-fit customers are those who:

- experience the relevant problem with enough urgency or frequency;
- use an alternative whose limits make the difference meaningful;
- possess the workflow, data, authority, collaborators, maturity, or infrastructure needed to realize value;
- recognize or can learn the evaluation criteria;
- can adopt, retain, and economically support the offer;
- do not require exceptions that erase the advantage.

Segmentation and positioning are reciprocal:

```text
customer differences
-> different value conditions or alternatives
-> segment hypothesis
-> positioning for that segment
-> acquisition, adoption, retention, and economic evidence
-> segment and position revision
```

Do not define the best-fit customer from firmographics alone. Use problem, alternative, use case, readiness, behavior, buying process, value realization, economics, and route-to-market evidence. See [Customer Segmentation & ICP](../customer-segmentation/README.md) for segment definitions and governance.

## Category Choice and Reframing

A category gives buyers a shortcut: what the product is, what it replaces, how it should be evaluated, and which budget or owner may apply.

| Choice | When it can work | Main cost |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Existing category | Buyers already use helpful criteria and the product's advantage is visible within them | Strong incumbents and inherited expectations |
| Subcategory or qualifier | The existing frame is mostly useful but one condition changes the best choice | The qualifier may be copied or ignored |
| Reframed category | Buyers understand the components but need a different evaluation hierarchy | Education, proof, and sales consistency |
| New category | Existing frames systematically hide the value and the company can sustain market education | Long time horizon, high capital and attention cost, uncertain adoption |

Category creation is not a naming exercise. It requires repeated education, credible examples, analyst and ecosystem understanding, customer proof, sales enablement, and enough market pull to make the new frame durable.

Test category comprehension, alternative recall, evaluation criteria, sales progression, and adoption expectations. A fashionable label that increases attention but attracts the wrong customers weakens positioning.

## Positioning Architecture

### Single Product

Use one position when buyers, alternatives, value, category, and proof are coherent. Segment-specific messages can still vary without creating separate positions.

### Platform and Product Portfolio

Choose deliberately among:

- independent product positions;
- a company umbrella with product-specific positions;
- one platform position supported by modules;
- a suite position centered on integrated value;
- segment-specific positions built from shared capabilities.

The architecture should clarify buying entry, shared value, cross-sell logic, proof, and product boundaries. An umbrella position fails when it becomes generic or asks every buyer to understand capabilities they do not need.

### Segment and Market Positions

Different segments may need different positions when their alternatives, value priorities, evaluation criteria, proof, or buying process materially differ. A translation or campaign variation alone does not establish a new position.

### Multi-Market Positioning

International expansion requires validating local alternatives, terminology, category understanding, channels, trust, regulation, price expectations, and proof. Preserve the core advantage where it remains true, but do not force global language onto markets with different decision contexts.

## Positioning Sequence

Work in dependency order:

1. Define the product, customer, buying situation, market, route, horizon, and owner.
2. Identify competitive alternatives, including status quo and inaction.
3. List currently available unique capabilities.
4. Translate each capability into differentiated value and proof.
5. Identify customers who care most and can realize the value.
6. Choose the category or reframing that makes the value easiest to understand.
7. Add trends only when they reinforce urgency or relevance.
8. Convert the position into messages, narrative, qualification, offer, and journey decisions.
9. Test behavior, retain contradictory evidence, and version the position.

Starting with a slogan, category, broad persona, or trend can lock the team into assumptions before it understands customer choice.

## Positioning Evidence Ledger

| Claim | Supporting evidence | Counterevidence | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Customers compare us with X | Win/loss, replacement behavior, sales and search language | Buyers repeatedly use a different frame | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Capability Y is meaningfully different | Product, technical, operational, or ecosystem proof | Alternatives match it in the relevant situation | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Difference Y creates value Z | Outcome data, customer language, avoided cost, behavior | Interest without realized or retained value | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Segment A is best fit | Urgency, conversion, activation, retention, willingness to pay | High service burden, weak retention, poor access | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Category C clarifies the decision | Comprehension, correct alternatives, evaluation behavior | Confusion, wrong expectations, unqualified demand | Verified, inferred, or missing |
| Proof P is credible | Repeatable demonstration, reference, audit, benchmark, outcome | Selection bias, unverifiable claim, incompatible context | Verified, inferred, or missing |

Record evidence date, unit, segment, source type, owner, and uncertainty. A position should state what is missing, not only what supports the preferred story.

## Positioning Workshop

Bring together product, sales, customer success, growth, market-facing teams, and leadership participants who hold different evidence. Before the session, collect recent win/loss, churn, customer language, product behavior, pricing, support, competitor, and market evidence.

The workshop should produce:

- one scoped positioning thesis;
- an alternatives map;
- capability-value-proof chains;
- best-fit and exclusion conditions;
- category decision and education burden;
- unresolved disagreements and evidence gaps;
- owners, validation work, version, and review date.

Do not use the session to negotiate a slogan. When functions disagree, identify which observation would resolve the disagreement and assign the work.

## Messaging Hierarchy

Messaging translates positioning for a specific audience and moment.

```text
positioning thesis
-> category and problem frame
-> differentiated value pillars
-> capability and proof
-> audience- and stage-specific messages
-> channel execution
```

Maintain a shared hierarchy so website, product, sales, lifecycle communication, partners, and customer success do not create conflicting evaluation criteria. Variants should change emphasis, proof, terminology, and detail without inventing a different product promise.

## Sales Narrative

A sales narrative helps the buyer understand the decision before presenting the product:

1. Market insight or change.
2. Existing alternatives and their limits.
3. Desired future state and decision criteria.
4. Differentiated value.
5. Product capabilities and credible proof.
6. Objections, tradeoffs, implementation, and next step.

The narrative should teach only what the evidence supports. A dramatic market story cannot compensate for weak value, and one narrative should not be forced across incompatible segments.

## Qualification and Discovery

Build qualification from the position. Test whether the prospect:

- has the relevant problem, trigger, and current alternative;
- experiences the constraint the product addresses;
- values the differentiated outcome;
- meets the prerequisites for realizing value;
- has an acceptable buying, implementation, governance, and economic path;
- can recognize credible proof and tradeoffs.

Discovery should capture contradictory evidence and disqualify poor-fit demand. Measure qualified progression, win/loss reasons, cycle length, discounting, implementation success, activation, renewal, and expansion by segment and promised use case.

## Translation Across the Growth System

| Area | Positioning decision | Evidence to monitor |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acquisition | Audience, problem frame, category, message, proof, and channel context | Qualified response, source quality, message-to-segment fit, downstream retention |
| Pricing and packaging | Value metric, package boundaries, willingness to pay, proof, and tradeoffs | Price acceptance, discounting, mix, margin, expansion, service cost |
| Sales | Qualification, discovery, narrative, objections, references, and competitive play | Progression, win/loss, cycle time, proof usage, no-decision rate |
| Activation | First value path and expectation set by the promise | Time to value, expected use case, setup success, early failure |
| Retention | Repeated value and customer conditions implied by the position | Use-case retention, renewal, expansion, contraction, churn reasons |
| Product | Capability priorities and proof-producing experience | Adoption, outcome quality, reliability, customer evidence |

If acquisition performs but activation or retention does not, investigate whether the message attracts the wrong customer or promises the wrong value. If retention is strong but sales is weak, inspect category comprehension, proof, qualification, and the buying path before changing the product thesis.

## AI Positioning, Trust, and Proof

“AI-powered” is a mechanism claim shared by many products. Strong AI positioning specifies:

- the alternative or workflow being replaced or improved;
- the task and customer for which the capability matters;
- output quality, reliability, control, latency, and cost conditions;
- proprietary context, data, workflow integration, feedback, or network advantages;
- privacy, security, compliance, audit, and human-review boundaries;
- demonstrations and outcomes that remain credible outside curated examples;
- how the position changes as models and competitors improve.

Trust is part of the value chain. Separate evidence of model capability, product reliability, workflow fit, governance, and customer outcome. A benchmark can support one link without proving the entire position.

## Positioning Test System

Test the position through a portfolio rather than one headline experiment.

| Test layer | Methods | Failure signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Alternatives | Interviews, win/loss, churn destinations, sales-call coding | Team and customer competitor sets disagree |
| Comprehension | First-click or five-second tasks, message interviews, category recall | Correct audience cannot explain what the product replaces |
| Value | Problem interviews, proof review, willingness-to-pay research | Interest does not translate into meaningful priority |
| Qualification | Lead review, discovery audits, segment progression | Volume increases while fit and downstream value decline |
| Sales | Narrative tests, objection analysis, competitive outcomes | More education produces longer cycles without better wins |
| Product | Promised-use-case activation and cohorts | Customers activate for a different reason or fail to realize proof |
| Economics | Price, package, discount, service, and contribution analysis | The best-understood position attracts uneconomic demand |

Message tests are supporting evidence. Validate the full chain through acquisition quality, buying behavior, realized value, retention, and economics.

## Measurement System

| Layer | Measures |
| --- | --- |
| Audience fit | Best-fit share of qualified demand, segment coverage, source mix, exclusion accuracy |
| Comprehension | Correct problem, alternative, category, value, and proof recall |
| Buying behavior | Qualified progression, no-decision, win/loss, sales cycle, objections, competitive displacement |
| Value realization | Promised-use-case activation, time to value, implementation success, outcome evidence |
| Commercial fit | Price acceptance, discounting, package mix, contribution, expansion, payback |
| Durable fit | Retention, renewal, contraction, churn reason, advocacy by promised use case |
| System quality | Evidence freshness, claim status, message consistency, enablement adoption, review completion |

Report rates and absolute contribution by positioning version and segment. Aggregate improvement can hide a position that works only for a small or uneconomic group.

## Diagnostic Map

| Observed pattern | Check next | Possible response |
| --- | --- | --- |
| High traffic, low qualified response | Audience, problem frame, category, and alternative | Narrow the position or change the acquisition context. |
| Strong lead volume, weak activation | Promise, qualification, prerequisites, and first value path | Align message and onboarding with realizable value. |
| Buyers understand features but not urgency | Status quo cost, trigger, differentiated outcome, proof | Strengthen the value chain or accept a longer education path. |
| Frequent “we already use X” losses | Actual alternative, switching cost, migration proof | Rebuild the comparison around the real decision. |
| High interest, repeated no-decision | Inaction, authority, risk, proof, implementation burden | Reduce uncertainty or prioritize higher-urgency situations. |
| Strong retention, weak sales conversion | Category, narrative, proof accessibility, buying roles | Improve decision framing before changing product value. |
| Sales wins, weak renewal | Overpromising, segment fit, implementation, value realization | Narrow claims and feed churn evidence into qualification. |
| One position fails across regions | Local alternatives, terminology, regulation, channel, trust | Create a market-specific version where differences are material. |
| Portfolio message is generic | Buying entry, shared value, product boundaries, cross-sell logic | Separate product positions or clarify platform architecture. |
| AI demos create trials but not retained use | Workflow fit, reliability, control, repeated value | Replace generic capability claims with proven use-case value. |

These patterns guide investigation and do not establish causality by themselves.

## Maturity Model

| Stage | Characteristics | Next constraint |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. Unscoped claims | Broad audience, feature list, generic benefits, slogan-led decisions | Define the unit, alternatives, and evidence status. |
| 2. Evidence-backed thesis | Alternatives, capabilities, value, best-fit customers, and category are explicit | Translate the thesis into proof, narrative, qualification, and journey decisions. |
| 3. Journey-aligned execution | Acquisition, sales, product, price, activation, and retention use a shared position | Measure by segment and retain contradictory evidence. |
| 4. Governed portfolio | Versions, owners, architectures, evidence ledgers, and review triggers are shared | Manage local and portfolio variation without losing coherence. |
| 5. Adaptive positioning system | Positions evolve with product, market, competition, model, and customer evidence | Preserve comparability, trust, and strategic focus while changing. |

Maturity is not the number of messages, persona documents, or brand guidelines. It is the ability to make and revise coherent product and growth decisions from evidence.

## Build Sequence

1. Choose one material product, segment, buying situation, and operating horizon.
2. Name the decision owner and collect customer, product, sales, pricing, retention, and competitive evidence.
3. Map direct, indirect, manual, status-quo, and inaction alternatives.
4. Build capability-consequence-value-proof chains with explicit boundaries.
5. Define best-fit conditions and exclusions with the segmentation system.
6. Choose the category and document the education burden and rejected frames.
7. Write one positioning thesis and supporting evidence ledger.
8. Translate it into messaging hierarchy, sales narrative, qualification, pricing, activation, and retention expectations.
9. Run comprehension, behavior, value, and economic tests with guardrails.
10. Approve a version, publish enablement, assign unresolved evidence work, and set the next review.

## Review Cadence and Governance

Review positioning on a regular operating cadence and when a trigger occurs:

- a new product, package, platform, segment, market, or route to market;
- changes in product capability, competition, regulation, price, or customer behavior;
- repeated win/loss, no-decision, activation, retention, or churn patterns;
- a category shift or new technology that changes buyer expectations;
- portfolio expansion, acquisition, consolidation, or brand architecture changes.

The review should ask:

- Which claims have new supporting or contradictory evidence?
- Are the best-fit customer and alternatives still correct?
- Does the category create the intended evaluation criteria?
- Are messages, sales, pricing, product, activation, and retention aligned?
- Which version is active for each scope, and who owns exceptions?
- What should be revised, tested, preserved, or retired?

Keep a decision log. Do not silently change positioning through isolated campaigns, sales decks, website copy, or feature launches.

## Guardrails

- Do not manufacture differentiation unsupported by product or operational evidence.
- Do not redefine a category only to avoid real competitors.
- Do not confuse a trend, technology, feature, slogan, or brand personality with customer value.
- Do not target customers who cannot realize the promised value.
- Do not let local messages create incompatible product promises.
- Do not attribute company growth to positioning without experiments or credible comparison.
- Do not treat a consultant, executive, or single team as the sole cause of a company outcome.
