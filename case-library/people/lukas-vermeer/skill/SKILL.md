# Lukas Vermeer Scalable Experimentation Lens

Apply Lukas Vermeer's publicly documented methods to experimentation systems and product operating models. Treat this as a method lens, not an impersonation, endorsement, or substitute for statistical, ethical, legal, or domain review.

## Establish the Context

Collect the decision, organization stage, product-team topology, experiment volume, eligible traffic, current workflow, platform capabilities, metric system, quality failures, central-team responsibilities, training level, concurrent exposure, and leadership incentives.

Keep unknowns explicit. Never invent experiment results, internal Booking.com or Vista practices, interaction rates, or Vermeer's opinion.

## Select the Lens

Read [frameworks.md](references/frameworks.md). Choose one primary lens and no more than two supporting lenses.

| Situation | Primary lens |
| --- | --- |
| Every test waits for a central team | Democratization with guardrails |
| Platform investment exceeds real usage | Constraint-led infrastructure |
| Teams fear any overlapping experiments | Concurrent-test design |
| Treatments may conflict | Interaction avoidance and detection |
| Adaptive allocation is proposed as default | Scientist versus bandit |
| Traffic is structurally low | Smaller-question evidence design |
| Central experts act as a service desk | Empowered-team ownership |
| Tests run but decisions do not change | Leadership and evidence culture |

## Build the Diagnosis

1. Name the decision and current experimentation constraint.
2. Map ownership across product teams, platform owners, analysts, reviewers, and leaders.
3. Separate mandatory validity guardrails from optional central approval.
4. Audit infrastructure, methodology, training, and culture as one capability system.
5. Classify concurrency risks by mechanism, shared surface, scarce resource, safety, and decision impact.
6. Choose mutual exclusion, exposure logging, interaction analysis, or ordinary overlap proportionately.
7. Check whether platform work answers observed demand or hypothetical scale.
8. Recommend the smallest operating change that improves trustworthy throughput.
9. Define decision, quality, adoption, and learning measures.

Do not equate decentralization with no governance, overlap with interaction, experiment count with learning, or platform sophistication with organizational capability.

## Deliver

Use this output order:

1. **Decision and current constraint**
2. **Team and ownership map**
3. **Infrastructure, methodology, training, and culture audit**
4. **Self-service and mandatory guardrails**
5. **Concurrency and interaction assessment**
6. **Scientist, bandit, or alternative evidence choice**
7. **Minimum viable operating change**
8. **Measures and review cadence**
9. **Risks, unknowns, and limitations**
10. **Sources**

Read [sources.md](references/sources.md) before citing. Distinguish Vermeer's direct statements from co-presenter material, interviewer framing, and company-wide outcomes.

## Required Boundary

Include this disclosure in substantial outputs:

> This analysis applies frameworks documented in Lukas Vermeer's public work. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or a simulation of Lukas Vermeer.

Never write as Lukas Vermeer, imitate his voice, imply private access, or claim he would make a decision.

## Common Mistakes

- Requiring central approval for every standard test.
- Removing quality controls in the name of democratization.
- Building for future scale before solving current adoption and trust constraints.
- Serializing all experiments because interaction is theoretically possible.
- Ignoring concurrent exposure when treatments share a mechanism or scarce surface.
- Using a bandit when the organization needs interpretable causal learning.
- Treating an underpowered result as proof of no effect.
- Making the experimentation team own product outcomes for other teams.
